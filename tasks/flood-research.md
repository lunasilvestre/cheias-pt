### January 2026 - The Storm Cluster

**Timeline:** Mid-to-late January 2026 experienced a catastrophic sequence of four Atlantic windstorms within weeks.

#### Storm Harry (Mid-January 2026)
- Type: Extratropical cyclone
- Mediterranean impact: Catastrophic
- Migrant boat incident: Multiple boats capsized (380+ deaths, NGO estimates 1,000)
- Secondary impacts: 2026 Gelida train derailment, 2026 Niscemi landslide
- **Component Tags:** FlagCard (official incident), Prose (human tragedy narrative)

#### Storm Ingrid (January 20-22, 2026)
- Named: January 20, 2026 by IPMA
- Coastal impact: Severe
- Wave heights: ~15 meters (toppled 40m cliffs in Portugal)
- Wind gusts: 100 km/h
- Alert level: Orange across most coastal districts
- Coastal damage: Notable erosion and structural damage
- **Component Tags:** CompareImage (before/after coastal erosion), Chart (wave height records)

#### Storm Joseph (January 25-27, 2026)
- Named: January 25, 2026 by IPMA
- Orange alerts: Porto, Lisbon, Braga districts
- Rainfall: Persistent, heavy
- Wind: High winds predicted
- River flooding risk: Particularly in the north
- Wave predictions: Up to 12 meters
- **Component Tags:** Widget (alert levels), Map (regional coverage)

#### Storm Kristin (January 28-30, 2026)
- **Status:** Most destructive of the winter cluster
- Named: January 28, 2026
- Landfall: Leiria district, January 28 evening
- Wind records:
  - Soure (Coimbra): 208.8 km/h (unprecedented record)
  - Leiria: 156 km/h
  - General: 202 km/h wind gusts
- Deaths: 6 direct, 8 indirect (14 total in Portugal)
  - Direct causes: Falling trees, structural collapses, woman swept away in Algarve
  - Indirect causes: Recovery operations, carbon monoxide poisoning (generators), 1 suicide
- Power outages: 1 million people
- Damage status: "Most damaging wind event since ex-hurricane Leslie (2018)"
- Economic impact: Expected to exceed Leslie's losses
- **Component Tags:** Chart (fatality timeline), StatisticWidget (wind records), Prose (personal stories), FlagCard (state of calamity declaration)

### February 2026 - Secondary Wave and Sustained Crisis

#### Storm Leonardo (February 3-6, 2026)
- **Status:** Second phase of compound disaster
- Trigger: Heavy, persistent rainfall on saturated landscapes
- Deaths: 1+ in Portugal, 1 in Spain (confirmed at least 2 total)
- Evacuations: 1,100 in Portugal, ~11,000 across Spain and Portugal
- Evacuations detail: ~200 residents in central Portugal as rivers rose
- Affected municipalities: Alcácer do Sal, Leiria (principal impacts)
- River impacts: Sado, Lis rivers overflowed
- Peak impact: February 5-6
- State of emergency: Extended by Prime Minister Luis Montenegro
- **Component Tags:** CompareImage (river levels before/after), Widget (evacuation statistics), Prose (survivor accounts), MapBlock (affected municipalities)

#### Tejo (Tagus) River Response
- Peak flow: 9,057 m³/s (February 6, 2026)
- Flow above 2,000 m³/s: Sustained during critical period
- Spanish dam releases: Alcántara and Cedillo dams released flows >7,000 m³/s
- Peak height: Highest level since 1997
- Affected region: Lezíria do Tejo (Azambuja, Benavente, Coruche)
- Recovery: By February 21, downgraded to blue alert; flow dropped to 1,498 m³/s
- Geographic extent: Salvaterra de Magos area: 64,000 hectares flooded (as of Feb 8)
- **Component Tags:** Chart (hydrograph with peak flow), MapBlock (inundation extent), Embed (SNIRH data if available)

#### Mondego River Response
- Alert escalation: Flood alert escalated in Mondego Valley
- Peak forecast: >1,500 m³/s (driven by Aguieira Dam releases + new rain)
- Dike rupture: Right-bank dike near Casais (February 11, 2026)
- Rupture cause: Flows exceeded 2,100 m³/s design threshold (infrastructure from 1980s)
- Secondary rupture: Granja do Ulmeiro (Soure)
- Evacuation scale: 3,000 evacuated from Coimbra area
- Affected municipalities: Soure, Montemor-o-Velho, Coimbra neighborhoods (Parque Verde)
- Infrastructure damage: A1 motorway collapse (see infrastructure section)
- **Component Tags:** CompareImage (satellite before/after dike rupture), Chart (discharge rates), FlagCard (A1 closure timeline)

#### Douro & Tâmega Rivers
- Status: Under surveillance during February crisis
- Douro: Burst banks in Porto and Vila Nova de Gaia (February 6, 2026)
- Tâmega: Closely monitored, posed overflow risk
- **Component Tags:** MapBlock (river network), Widget (alert status tracking)

#### Sado River
- Status: Flooded Alcácer do Sal severely
- Water rise: 2 meters in 20 minutes (eyewitness accounts)
- Historic comparison: Residents noted worst flooding in 30 years
- **Component Tags:** Prose (eyewitness testimony), Chart (rate of rise)

#### Storm Marta (February 6-8, 2026)
- **Status:** Third successive storm in compound disaster
- Deaths: 2+ (continued accumulation)
- Displacing: 11,000+ additional residents
- Precipitation: 60mm in 24 hours (Alentejo, Algarve mountains expected)
- Wind: Up to 120 km/h
- Snow: Reported in upland areas
- Sea state: Waves up to 13 meters; entire coastline on orange alert
- Alert distribution: 8 of 18 mainland districts on orange alert
- Response: 26,500 rescuers deployed
- Electoral impact: 3 municipalities postponed Sunday presidential vote
- **Component Tags:** Widget (alert summary), Chart (rescuer deployment scale), Prose (emergency response narrative)

### 7. CLIMATE FRAMING

**Historical Comparison: 1967 Tagus Flood**

| Aspect | 1967 Flood | 2026 Winter Flooding |
|--------|-----------|---|
| Type | Flash flood (5-hour concentration) | Multi-week sustained flooding |
| Area of origin | Metro Lisbon (14 municipalities) | Central + Southern Portugal (69 municipalities) |
| Rainfall intensity | >120mm in 5-hour window | 222% of normal over month |
| Water rise (Tagus) | 3-4 meters (Lisbon) | Peak flow 9,057 m³/s (vs. typical) |
| Deaths | 500-700 (deadliest in 100 years) | 18-22 |
| Economic damage | N/A (pre-modern estimates) | €4+ billion |
| Area affected | Metropolitan zone | Dispersed across multiple regions |
| Saturation factor | Dry soils | Already saturated (compound disaster) |
| Fatality concentration | Night of Nov 25-26 | Spread over 4+ weeks |

**Key insight:** 2026 was geographically broader and economically more damaging; 1967 was more concentrated and deadly per capita.

**Climate Attribution Research:**

**World Weather Attribution Study Findings:**
- Carbon emissions caused 11% increase in rainfall intensity (northern region)
- 36% increase in extreme one-day rainfall (southern region)
- 29% increase in extreme one-day rainfall (northern region)
- Storm Leonardo: One-day totals at "once-in-a-century" frequency (currently; will increase)
- Attribution: Human-caused climate change "intensified" these winter downpours

**Long-term Projections for Portugal:**
- Annual precipitation: Declining 15% (north) to 30% (south)
- Seasonal trend: Decrease in all seasons except winter
- Intensity: Extreme rainfall events (high-intensity days) will increase substantially
- Implication: More droughts interspersed with intense flood events

**Policy relevance:** Climate attribution strengthens case for PGRI flood risk plans and EU Floods Directive compliance.

**Component Tags:** Chart (climate attribution percentages), Prose (attribution science narrative), Embed (World Weather Attribution report link)

### 8. POLICY & INFRASTRUCTURE RESPONSE

**Existing Framework:**
- PGRI (Plano de Gestão de Risco de Inundação): Portuguese flood risk management plan
- EU Floods Directive: Implementation framework for transboundary risk
- SNIRH: Real-time hydrological monitoring
- Copernicus: Satellite-based emergency mapping

**Critical Infrastructure Failures:**

**A1 Motorway Collapse (Mondego):**
- Location: Near Coimbra, pillar base
- Trigger: Dike rupture on February 11, 2026
- Cause: Flows exceeded 2,100 m³/s (design threshold from 1980s)
- Impact: Main transport corridor between Lisbon and Porto severed
- Status: Expected to be out of service "several weeks"
- Advance closure: Road closed before collapse; no casualties
- Economic impact: Major component of €4B+ total

**Urban Drainage Failures:**
- Pattern: Excessive construction without drainage/retention basins
- Problem statement: "No room for water" (Euronews characterization)
- Timeline of saturation: Oct 1, 2025 - Feb 16, 2026 = 1,238mm in Coimbra (record)
- 1970s infrastructure: Designed for different climate; inadequate for current extremes

**Planned Infrastructure Improvements:**

1. **Lisbon General Drainage Plan**
   - Initiated: 2016
   - Budget: €250 million
   - Components: Retention basins, two tunnels (Monsanto-Sta Apolónia, Chelas-Beato)
   - Status: Ongoing

2. **Mondego Basin Overhaul (Post-2026)**
   - Lead: Agência Portuguesa do Ambiente (APA)
   - Scope: Comprehensive flood management system review
   - Funding: EU climate adaptation funds (potentially available)
   - Proposed elements:
     - Taller, reinforced dikes (Lower Mondego)
     - Supplementary retention basins upstream
     - New upstream control dam

3. **Order of Engineers Technical Report**
   - Commission: Ministry of Environment and Energy
   - Focus: Detailed 2026 flood analysis and recommendations
   - Urgency: High priority

**Component Tags:** FlagCard (infrastructure project timelines), Chart (budget allocation), Prose (policy response narrative), Embed (PGRI and EU directive links)

## RESEARCH NOTES & CAVEATS

1. **Data sourcing:** All information sourced from open web searches; primary sources include official government statements, news coverage, and scientific attribution studies.

2. **Death toll variation:** Different sources cite 14-22 total deaths depending on whether indirect causes and maritime incidents are included. This analysis uses 18 as a conservative documented count.

3. **Economic damage estimate:** Range of €3-4.6 billion cited; €4 billion used as middle estimate. Final accounting may differ.

4. **EMSR codes:** EMSR861 and EMSR864 confirmed; other potential activations may exist and should be verified via Copernicus portal.

5. **State of calamity municipalities:** Sources cite 60-69 municipalities; research indicates gradual expansion from initial Kristin declaration through Leonardo and Marta phases.

6. **Satellite data:** Specific acquisition dates and image availability should be verified directly through Copernicus/ESA archives.

7. **Comparative 1967 data:** Historical 1967 flood well-documented but pre-digital; some measurements are estimates.

8. **Attribution study:** World Weather Attribution study is ongoing; preliminary findings cited reflect early analysis.

9. **Agricultural damage:** €750M estimate is official government figure; total crop losses may be higher when including indirect market impacts.

10. **Future timelines:** A1 motorway repair and infrastructure project timelines are estimates and subject to change based on conditions and funding.
